The Moon is unlike any other major celestial object we know.

It’s perfectly tidally locked — rotating exactly once per orbit — so that one face always looks toward Earth. To most observers, it appears motionless, as though fixed in the sky. Scientists call it “synchronous rotation,” or tidal locking. Over billions of years, Earth’s gravity gradually slowed the Moon’s spin until it matched its orbital period. This has happened to other moons in the solar system, but the Moon’s massive size relative to Earth, and its precise 1:1 ratio, makes it stand out.
